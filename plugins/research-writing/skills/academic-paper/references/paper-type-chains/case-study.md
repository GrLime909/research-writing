# Paper Type: Case Study

Corresponds to `paper_structure_patterns.md` Pattern 4.

## Argument Chain

`context -> case selection rationale -> analysis framework -> findings -> cross-theme synthesis -> transferability -> lessons learned`

## Drafting Order

1. **Case description** — context, setting, key actors, timeline, data sources. Write descriptively first.
2. **Analysis framework** — how findings will be organized (thematic, chronological, comparative).
3. **Findings** — theme by theme, each with evidence from the case.
4. **Discussion** — cross-theme synthesis, comparison with literature, theoretical insights.
5. **Lessons learned** — for practitioners, policymakers, researchers.
6. **Conclusion** — concise synthesis.

## Core Principles

- **Evidence-first**: describe the case thoroughly before analyzing it. The reader must trust the data before accepting the interpretation.
- **Transferability, not generalizability**: a case study does not claim statistical generalization. Instead, provide enough contextual detail for readers to judge whether findings transfer to their own setting.
- **Case selection must be justified**: convenience sampling is weak. Theoretical, purposive, or extreme-case sampling must be explicitly argued.

## Analysis Section Structure

Each finding/theme:

1. State the finding.
2. Present evidence from the case (documents, interviews, observations, data).
3. Interpret the finding in relation to the research question.
4. Connect to relevant literature.

## Cross-Theme Synthesis

After presenting individual themes, synthesize across them:

1. What patterns emerge across themes?
2. How do the themes interact or contradict each other?
3. What does the synthesis reveal that individual themes do not?

## Common Pitfalls

- Describing the case without analyzing it (narrative without interpretation).
- Claiming generalizability from a single or small-N case.
- Omitting the case selection rationale.
- Presenting findings without connecting them back to the research question.
- Lessons learned that are generic rather than grounded in the case evidence.
