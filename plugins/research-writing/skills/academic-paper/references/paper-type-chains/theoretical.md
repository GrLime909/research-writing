# Paper Type: Theoretical Analysis

Corresponds to `paper_structure_patterns.md` Pattern 3.

## Argument Chain

`phenomenon -> theoretical gap -> proposed extension -> supporting arguments -> rival perspectives -> bounded conclusion`

## Drafting Order

1. **Background** — the theory in context: origins, key concepts, current state.
2. **Critical analysis** — strengths, limitations, competing perspectives, empirical support.
3. **Proposed extension** — new dimensions, conceptual model, propositions.
4. **Applications** — theoretical, practical, methodological implications.
5. **Conclusion** — summary of contribution, limitations of the analysis, future directions.

## Core Principles

- State the theoretical gap explicitly and locatably. Do not bury it in the third paragraph of the Background.
- Distinguish "supporting" arguments from "consistent but non-discriminating" arguments. Many drafts conflate them.
- Address competing theoretical frameworks **before** generalizing. Skipping this is the most common rejection point for theoretical papers.
- Hedging must match argumentative strength. Analogical evidence cannot ground causal mechanism verbs (`drives`, `causes`, `determines`).

## Propositions for Testing

When proposing extensions, state propositions clearly enough to be testable:

1. Define the proposition.
2. Specify boundary conditions.
3. Indicate what evidence would support or refute it.

## Results vs Interpretation

Keep Background to established theoretical state and empirical findings. All proposed extensions and interpretations go in the Extension/Analysis sections. Reviewers will check this split.

## Common Pitfalls

- Theoretical gap is asserted but not demonstrated against existing frameworks.
- Proposed extension is described at a high level without testable propositions.
- Competing perspectives are ignored or dismissed without engagement.
- Hedging language does not match the strength of the supporting arguments.
