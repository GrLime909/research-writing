# Paper Type: Thematic Literature Review

Corresponds to `paper_structure_patterns.md` Pattern 2.

## Argument Chain

`scope statement -> organizing principle (mechanism / method / application) -> synthesis grouped by argument -> disagreements and gaps -> author's stance with reasoning -> outlook with informative open questions`

## Drafting Order

1. **Scope definition** — define sub-area, time window, inclusion criteria precisely.
2. **Organizing principle** — choose the mechanism by which themes are grouped (by method, by application domain, by theoretical lens).
3. **Theme sections** — synthesize, do not survey.
4. **Cross-cutting synthesis** — convergent findings, divergent findings, methodological observations.
5. **Research gaps and future directions** — identified gaps, proposed research agenda.
6. **Conclusion** — key takeaways and implications.

## Core Principle

**A review is not a survey list.** Replace `Author A reported X. Author B reported Y.` with synthesis grouped by mechanism, method, or conclusion.

## Theme Section Structure

Each theme section should:

1. Define the theme scope.
2. Present representative work grouped by sub-theme.
3. Synthesize convergent and divergent findings.
4. Identify what remains unresolved in this theme.
5. Connect the unresolved point back to the overall review argument.

## Writing Rules

- Define scope precisely. Vague scopes invite reviewer pushback.
- Position the reviewer's own stance carefully. A review can take a view, but must show reasoning, not assert it.
- Use connectives that signal logical relation: `in contrast`, `building on this`, `the remaining disagreement is`. Avoid generic `furthermore`, `additionally`.
- The closing section should leave the reader with a usable map of the field, not a summary of what was just read.

## Common Pitfalls

- Listing papers chronologically without thematic organization.
- Describing each paper in isolation without cross-paper synthesis.
- Failing to state the reviewer's own stance and reasoning.
- Generic future directions ("more research is needed") instead of informative open questions.
